Mythos Revisited: Archeological promenade in Dipylon, Athens



Status:     Academic-Architectural Design 6

Program:     Archeological museum and facilities lab

Year:     2018

Location:     Pireos 77, Athens, Greece

Team:     Panagiotis Varoutsos, Konstantina Demiri



The main idea of this project was to create an architectural promenade with supporting architectural facilities for the archeological site of Dipylon in Athens. The Dipylon (Greek: Δίπυλον, “Two-Gated”) was the main gate in the city wall of Classical Athens. Located in the modern suburb of Kerameikos, it led to the namesake ancient cemetery, and to the roads connecting Athens with the rest of Greece. Now, only a few ruins remain on site, with great historical and cultural value. Accessing the architectural proposal through the main road of Pireos, the visitor enters a complex metallic structure consisting of wide staircases that ascend and descend both physically and metaphorically into time and space. Architecture here serves as a literal threshold, composing a building-passage that connects through upward and downward movements the present and the past. Continuing this architectural promenade from the main central passage, they come across a public café and the supporting labs that supplement the functions of the archeological museum nearby. The purpose is to provide a more performative experience that resembles a form of initiation in the space and thus the materials used is concrete and corten in respect to the landscape and the earthly tones of its surrounding
